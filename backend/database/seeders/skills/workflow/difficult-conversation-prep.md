# Difficult Conversation Prep

Prepare for conversations where stakes are high and emotions may run strong. Structure your thinking, anticipate the other person's perspective, and develop responses that keep the conversation productive.

## What This Orchestrates

1. Context gathering (what happened, why this is hard)
2. Talking points development (what you need to say)
3. Anticipated responses (how they might react)
4. Reframes (how to redirect unproductive patterns)
5. Opening and closing strategies

## Process

### 1. Gather Context

Understand the situation and your goals:

**The facts:**

- What happened or needs to be discussed?
- Who is involved?
- What's the history?

**Why this is difficult:**

- What makes you uncomfortable?
- What's at stake for you? For them?
- What emotions are likely present (yours and theirs)?

**Your goals:**

- What outcome do you want?
- What's the minimum acceptable outcome?
- What relationship do you want after?

### 2. Understand Their Perspective

Before preparing your points, consider theirs:

- How might they see the situation?
- What are their likely concerns or fears?
- What do they probably want from this conversation?
- What constraints are they operating under?

This isn't about agreeing with them. It's about not being surprised.

### 3. Develop Talking Points

Structure what you need to communicate:

**Core message:** One sentence capturing what you need them to understand.

**Supporting points:**

- Facts that support your perspective
- Impact statements (what happened, not blame)
- Specific examples (not generalizations)

**What to avoid:**

- Character judgments ("You always..." / "You never...")
- Mind-reading ("You obviously don't care about...")
- Kitchen-sinking (bringing up unrelated issues)

### 4. Anticipate Responses

For each likely response type, prepare:

**If they get defensive:**

- Acknowledge their perspective first
- Return to facts and impact
- Avoid escalating

**If they deflect or blame:**

- Note it without fighting
- Redirect to the specific issue
- Don't take the bait

**If they shut down:**

- Give space
- Offer to pause and return
- State what you still need to resolve

**If they escalate:**

- Stay calm, lower your voice
- Take a break if needed
- Don't match intensity

### 5. Prepare Reframes

When conversation goes sideways, have redirects ready:

| Unproductive pattern  | Reframe                                                         |
| --------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "You're attacking me" | "I'm trying to solve this together, not blame you"              |
| "This isn't fair"     | "Help me understand what feels unfair"                          |
| "You do it too"       | "We can discuss that separately. Right now I need to address X" |
| "This is pointless"   | "What would make this conversation useful for you?"             |

Prepare 2-3 specific reframes based on likely patterns for this person.

### 6. Plan Opening and Closing

**Opening:**

- State purpose clearly and calmly
- Set collaborative tone
- Signal this is important but not adversarial

Example: "I want to talk about [topic]. This matters to me and I'd like us to work through it together."

**Closing options:**

- If resolved: Summarize agreements, confirm next steps
- If unresolved: Acknowledge progress made, agree on when to continue
- If stuck: State what you need, propose next step, end respectfully

## Adapts To

**Conversation type:**

- Feedback delivery: Focus on specific behaviors, impact, and path forward
- Conflict resolution: Emphasize both perspectives, finding common ground
- Negotiation: Clarify interests, know your BATNA, prepare concessions
- Boundary setting: Clear statement of what you need, consequences
- Difficult news: Empathy first, clarity on facts, support offered

**Relationship context:**

- Manager to direct report: Authority balanced with support
- Peer to peer: Mutual respect, no power dynamics
- Report to manager: Advocating up, being heard
- External (client, vendor): Maintaining relationship while addressing issue

**Emotional intensity:**

- Hot (recent, raw): More emphasis on de-escalation, may need cooling period
- Warm (ongoing tension): Balance addressing history with forward focus
- Cool (preventive): Can be more direct, less emotional management

**Stakes level:**

- Career-affecting: More preparation, consider having witness or documenting
- Relationship-affecting: Preserve connection while addressing issue
- Project-affecting: Focus on outcomes and path forward

## Quality Checks

- [ ] Goals are clear (know what success looks like)
- [ ] Their perspective considered genuinely (not strawmanned)
- [ ] Talking points are specific (examples, not generalizations)
- [ ] Anticipated responses based on this person's patterns
- [ ] Reframes are de-escalating (not clever comebacks)
- [ ] Opening sets collaborative tone
- [ ] Closing accounts for multiple outcomes
- [ ] Preparation matches stakes (not over/under-prepared)
