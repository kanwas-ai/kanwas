# SBI Feedback

Structure feedback using the Situation-Behavior-Impact framework to make it specific, non-judgmental, and actionable.

## The Methodology

**Source:** Center for Creative Leadership (CCL)

The SBI model separates observation from interpretation:

| Component     | Definition                       | Key Question                       |
| ------------- | -------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| **Situation** | Specific time, place, context    | When and where did this happen?    |
| **Behavior**  | Observable actions, not motives  | What did you actually see or hear? |
| **Impact**    | Effect on you, team, or outcomes | What was the result?               |

### Why It Works

- Removes judgment and interpretation
- Grounds feedback in specifics
- Makes it easier to receive and act on
- Separates the person from the behavior

## Process

### 1. Gather Input

Ask for:

- What happened (the situation they want to address)
- What the person did (the behavior)
- What effect it had (the impact)
- Whether this is reinforcing (positive) or redirecting (constructive) feedback

### 2. Identify the Situation

Extract specific context:

- When: Date, time, or timeframe
- Where: Meeting, Slack, code review, etc.
- What was happening: The setting or circumstance

**Red flags to fix:**

- "You always..." → Find a specific instance
- "Recently..." → Pin down when exactly
- "In general..." → Pick one concrete example

### 3. Describe the Behavior

Focus on observable actions:

- What they said (quote if possible)
- What they did (visible actions)
- What they produced (tangible output)

**Red flags to fix:**

- "You were disrespectful" → What did they actually say/do?
- "You didn't care" → What observable behavior showed that?
- "You were unprofessional" → What specific action?

### 4. Articulate the Impact

State the consequence:

- Effect on you: "I felt..." or "I was unable to..."
- Effect on the team: "The team couldn't..."
- Effect on outcomes: "This caused..." or "This enabled..."

**Red flags to fix:**

- No impact stated → Ask: "So what? Why does this matter?"
- Assumed impact → Stick to what you actually observed

### 5. Draft the Statement

Combine into a natural statement:

**Reinforcing (positive):**

> "During [situation], when you [behavior], it [impact]."

**Redirecting (constructive):**

> "In [situation], when you [behavior], the impact was [impact]. Going forward, I'd like to see [alternative]."

### 6. Review for Judgment

Check the draft for:

- Interpretations disguised as facts
- Loaded words (lazy, careless, unprofessional)
- Mind-reading ("you didn't think about...")
- Absolutes (always, never)

Remove or rephrase any found.

## Before/After Example

**Before (vague, judgmental):**

> "You're always late to meetings and it's really disrespectful to the team. You need to be more professional."

**After (SBI structured):**

> "In yesterday's sprint planning meeting, you arrived 15 minutes after the scheduled start time. When that happened, we had to pause and recap what we'd already discussed, which added 10 minutes to the meeting and meant we didn't get to the last two items on the agenda. Going forward, can you prioritize being on time, or let me know in advance if you'll be late so we can adjust?"

**Why the second version works better:**

- Specific situation (yesterday's sprint planning)
- Observable behavior (arrived 15 minutes late)
- Concrete impact (recap added time, missed agenda items)
- Clear ask (be on time or communicate)
- No judgment words (disrespectful, unprofessional)

## Quality Checks

Before delivering the output, verify:

- [ ] **Situation is specific** — Not "you always" or "in general"
- [ ] **Behavior is observable** — Someone else could have seen/heard it
- [ ] **Impact is stated** — Clear consequence, not assumed
- [ ] **No judgment language** — No loaded words or interpretations
- [ ] **Tone matches intent** — Reinforcing feels appreciative, redirecting feels constructive
- [ ] **Actionable** — For redirecting feedback, there's a clear ask

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake                    | Example                       | Fix                                                                   |
| -------------------------- | ----------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Situation too vague        | "Lately in meetings..."       | Pin down one specific meeting                                         |
| Behavior is interpretation | "You were dismissive"         | "You said 'that won't work' and moved on"                             |
| Impact is assumed          | "It upset the team"           | "I noticed Sarah stopped contributing" or own it: "I felt frustrated" |
| Adding judgment            | "...which was unprofessional" | Cut it—the impact speaks for itself                                   |
| Sandwiching                | Praise + criticism + praise   | Keep feedback focused on one thing                                    |

## Adapting for Context

**Written feedback (email, performance review):**

- Can include multiple SBI instances as examples
- Be extra careful about tone—no vocal cues

**Verbal feedback (1:1, in the moment):**

- Prepare but don't read from a script
- Leave space for their response
- The SBI statement opens dialogue, not closes it

**Peer feedback:**

- Focus on "I" impacts (how it affected you)
- Avoid speaking for others

**Manager feedback:**

- Can include team/org impacts
- Connect to expectations or standards when relevant
