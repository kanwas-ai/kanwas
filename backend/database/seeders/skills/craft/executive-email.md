# Executive Email

Craft emails that respect executive time and get decisions made.

## What Good Looks Like

- Bottom line in first sentence
- 3 sentences max for the ask
- Clear decision or action needed
- Context without over-explaining
- Easy to respond to (yes/no when possible)
- Every word earns its place

## Process

1. **Identify the core ask** — What decision or action do you need?
2. **Write the bottom line first** — Lead with the answer, not the journey
3. **Add minimal context** — Only what's needed to decide
4. **Make response easy** — Clear options, yes/no when possible
5. **Cut ruthlessly** — If it doesn't help them decide, delete it

## Example

### Before (Original Draft)

> Hi Sarah,
>
> I wanted to reach out about the Q2 planning session. As you know, we've been working on the roadmap for the past few weeks and the team has made great progress. We've identified several priorities and have been debating the best approach. There are some dependencies with the infrastructure team that we need to sort out, and I've been talking to Mike about timelines.
>
> I think it would be really helpful if we could get your input on the direction. The team is leaning toward Option A (focus on enterprise features) but Option B (platform stability) also has merit. We've done some analysis and both have trade-offs.
>
> Would you have time to discuss? I'm flexible on timing and can work around your schedule. Let me know what works.
>
> Thanks!

### After (Executive Version)

> Sarah,
>
> Need your call on Q2 priority: enterprise features vs platform stability.
>
> Team recommends enterprise features — higher revenue impact, but delays stability work by one quarter.
>
> Can you approve enterprise focus, or should we discuss trade-offs? 15 min this week if needed.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Burying the ask** — Don't make them hunt for what you need
- **Excessive context** — They can ask if they need more
- **Open-ended questions** — "What do you think?" forces more work than "A or B?"
- **Hedging language** — "I was wondering if maybe..." wastes their time
- **Calendar deference** — "Whenever works for you" pushes scheduling work to them

## Quality Checks

- [ ] Bottom line appears in first sentence
- [ ] Total email is under 100 words
- [ ] Ask is explicit (what do you need from them?)
- [ ] Response options are clear
- [ ] Could they respond in 30 seconds?
- [ ] No unnecessary pleasantries or filler
- [ ] Every sentence advances the ask
